## Overview

<cite index="4-3">
  An upright backpacking canister stove released by MSR in 2019, the
  PocketRocket Deluxe takes the normal version of their popular upright canister
  stove and adds an external piezo push-start ignition system along with an
  internal pressure regulator, promising more stable performance across both
  low-canister and cold-weather applications.
</cite>
<cite index="11-4">
  For backpackers traveling fast and light on the trail, this deluxe model
  offers one of the best overall cooking experiences in its ultralight class.
</cite>
It's also a stove that actual thru-hikers reach for:
<cite index="3-5">
  the Deluxe was the lightweight backpacking stove used by nearly every
  thru-hiker encountered on one John Muir Trail thru-hike.
</cite>

## Performance

### Boil Speed

The Deluxe earns its stripes out of the gate on raw speed. <cite index="12-33">In Outdoor Gear Lab's testing, it boiled 1 liter of water in 3 minutes and 14 seconds — an unbeatable score that couldn't be bested even by integrated canister stoves.</cite> In real-world use, <cite index="4-15">the stove can perform a 2-cup boil in under 2 minutes, with test times ranging from 1:59 to 2:01.</cite> That's fast enough that you'll want your freeze-dried dinner open and ready before you strike the igniter.

### Pressure Regulation & Cold Weather

<cite index="3-4">
  From its piezo igniter to its pressure regulator — which ensures a consistent
  flame even in the cold or when the fuel canister is low — this feature-packed
  stove delivers a lot in a compact package.
</cite>
<cite index="4-16">
  As long as you have a full canister, your boil times won't change much between
  cooking dinner in the warm evening and getting water boiling on a chilly
  morning.
</cite>
That said,
<cite index="4-18,4-19">
  the stove's performance did fall off when canisters were low — noticeably so
  in moderate conditions, and quite a bit in cold conditions, as one would
  expect for all upright canister stoves.
</cite>
One REI reviewer reported
<cite index="14-10">
  having a hard time getting a rolling boil at 30°F in the tundra of Alaska
  despite a new canister.
</cite>
The regulator helps, but it's not a magic bullet in true alpine winter
conditions — that's what a remote-canister or liquid-fuel stove is for.

### Wind Resistance

The broad, recessed burner head is a genuine upgrade over the standard PocketRocket 2. <cite index="5-5">It can bring water to a rolling boil even in an 8–10 mph wind</cite> — something most non-integrated canister stoves can't claim. <cite index="5-19,5-20">Only integrated canister stoves boil faster with wind present.</cite> The trade-off is fuel: <cite index="5-16">in wind testing, the Deluxe burned 0.83 oz of fuel to boil one liter of water</cite>, compared to 0.52 oz in calm conditions. Sustained wind will chew through your canister. <cite index="4-20,4-21">There's also a relatively large gap between the burner head and the bottom of the pot since the pot supports raise your cookware quite high, creating a fairly significant area where wind can decrease performance.</cite> Seek a wind break when the gusts get serious.

### Simmer Control

This is where the Deluxe really separates itself from a basic canister stove. <cite index="7-18,7-19">Simmer quality and raw output are quite good — in terms of flame control, it's easily one of the finest canister stoves around, integrated systems included.</cite> <cite index="12-24">The control valve has the right amount of resistance, making it easy to dial in the correct amount of heat.</cite> <cite index="6-25,6-26,6-27,6-28">One of the older PocketRocket's notable faults was its inability to simmer — cooking rice, for example, was nearly impossible without burning it. The pressure regulator on the Deluxe is a significant improvement in that regard.</cite> It still runs a little hot at the low end compared to a kitchen range, but it's genuinely usable for actual cooking, not just boiling.

### Igniter Reliability

The Piezo is one of the Deluxe's marquee features, and it mostly delivers. <cite index="18-9">In field use, the PocketRocket Deluxe lit reliably using its Piezo igniter — one reviewer didn't have to use a backup Bic lighter even once, including in wind.</cite> <cite index="3-27,3-28">Tossed into a mesh pocket or inside a cookpot, often with drops of hot water, the piezo kept working and didn't get crushed or destroyed.</cite> That said, altitude can be a problem: at least one REI reviewer reported <cite index="14-33">the piezo igniter completely failing to ignite the stove upon reaching a trailhead at 10,000 feet.</cite> <cite index="12-27,12-28">Some variability in igniter reliability has been noted — which is why carrying a lighter is still a smart habit, even with a stove that has an integrated igniter.</cite> Treat the piezo as a convenience, not a guarantee.

### Build Quality & Ease of Use

<cite index="4-1">
  Overall construction is quite solid and despite the light weight, nothing on
  this stove feels flimsy.
</cite>
<cite index="3-18,3-19,3-20,3-21">
  The pot-stand arms fold in two directions — up to hold a pot and down to stow
  away — and don't fold on themselves like some competitors' designs. This means
  fewer parts to break or get stuck, and setup is simple.
</cite>
One note on quality control:
<cite index="1-18,1-19">
  some units have been reported with a considerably loose pot-holder leg — two
  legs with good tolerance and one so loose it was hard to keep in position.
</cite>
Check all three legs are locked before setting a pot down.

## Pros & Cons

**Pros**

- Pressure regulator delivers consistent performance across canister fill levels and mild cold
- Among the fastest boil times in its class — regulated or not
- Excellent simmer control, genuinely usable for real cooking
- <cite index="3-23,3-24">
    Durable and reliable without feeling overbuilt; the simple design doesn't
    have delicate parts that can snag or break off
  </cite>
- Can hold a rolling boil in moderate wind — a real differentiator among upright canister stoves
- Compact enough to nest inside most 1L cookpots

**Cons**

- Piezo igniter can fail at altitude; carry a lighter regardless
- <cite index="7-31,7-32">
    Compared directly to an integrated stove in sustained wind, the PocketRocket
    compares poorly in both boil times and fuel consumption
  </cite>
- <cite index="10-42">
    The stove sits noticeably higher than its predecessor when mounted on a
    larger canister
  </cite>
  , reducing stability — especially on uneven ground
- Some units have shown pot-support leg tolerance issues
- <cite index="6-13,6-14">
    The PocketRocket 2 weighs just 10 grams less — for those 10 extra grams you
    get the igniter, regulator, and broader burner — but if you're counting
    every gram, the difference is real
  </cite>
- Canister fuel isn't allowed on planes and can be hard to source internationally

## Who Should Buy This

<cite index="12-5,12-6">
  With its lightweight profile, excellent simmering capabilities, and compact
  design, the PocketRocket Deluxe opens up real cooking options for ultralight
  backpackers; it's also decently fuel-efficient and can boil water quickly in
  the wind.
</cite>
It's the right call for three-season thru-hikers, JMT-style trekkers, and anyone
who wants to cook actual food — not just rehydrate a pouch.
<cite index="7-25">
  If you specifically prefer to use a stand-alone canister stove, either for
  weight savings or cooking flexibility, the PocketRocket Deluxe is clearly
  among the best in its class.
</cite>
If you're frequently camping in sustained high wind or genuinely sub-freezing
temperatures, a windshield system or a remote-canister stove like the MSR
WindBurner will serve you better.

## Verdict

The PocketRocket Deluxe is as close to a "default recommendation" as the canister stove category has. <cite index="3-2,3-3">After extended testing, it earns its place among the best lightweight backpacking stoves out there — excelling from boil time to simmer mode and standing out for ease of use and durability.</cite> The pressure regulator and broad burner genuinely move the needle over the PocketRocket 2 at a weight penalty of just 10 grams. Carry a backup lighter, check your pot-support legs, and this stove will quietly get out of your way and let you eat well on the trail. **Rating: 8.5/10.**

## Overview

The TOAKS POT-550-L is about as close to the platonic ideal of an ultralight solo cook pot as you'll find. <cite index="10-1">It's aimed at solo ultralight adventurers who want a small pot that doubles as a cup</cite> — and at 72g for the complete pot-and-lid system, it earns that position comfortably. It's been a community staple on r/Ultralight and Backpacking Light for years, and the reasons aren't hard to see: dead-simple design, a price that won't make you wince, and enough capacity to handle the meals that actually matter on trail.

## Performance

### Capacity and Cooking

<cite index="21-12">
  The 550 holds 550 milliliters — about 18.5 oz — of fluid to the brim, so when
  you factor in a little room, at max capacity it's workable for the 2 cups many
  freeze-dried meals call for and about right for a big cup of morning coffee.
</cite>
That's the functional ceiling.
<cite index="18-18">
  In practice, 550ml is filled to the point of overflowing, so realistically
  it's about 500ml.
</cite>
For a boil-and-pour backpacking style — freeze-dried meals, instant coffee,
ramen — that's exactly enough. If you're hoping to actually cook anything in
there,
<cite index="16-7">
  beware: it won't fit a full ramen and definitely doesn't fit a box of mac n'
  cheese.
</cite>

### Heat Performance

Titanium doesn't distribute heat as evenly as aluminum, and the 0.3mm wall means there's essentially nothing slowing the transfer from stove to hand. The flip side is that it heats up fast. <cite index="6-4">On an Esbit stove, water heats up very quickly in the TOAKS 550.</cite> The thin walls also mean it cools off fast — <cite index="10-4,10-5">when hot, use a towel or gloves to grab the pot or lid handles; or wait just a little bit, as titanium cools off quickly.</cite> The handles themselves have a finger-rest notch which helps with grip, but they transfer heat readily with no insulation. A silicone band or a piece of grip tape on the handles is a worthwhile addition if you drink directly from this as a mug.

### Lid

<cite index="6-11,6-12">
  The triangular grab ring on the top of the lid can be slid to the side to keep
  it locked upright — a handy feature if you're heating contents and want to
  check on cooking progress without burning your fingers.
</cite>
The lid is also vented, which lets steam escape without a lid-blowing pressure
event. One honest note:
<cite index="28-10">
  the lid is designed to fit loosely, which prevents vacuum lock after boiling
  for easier, safer handling
</cite>
— so if you pick this up expecting a tight, rattling seal, you won't get it.
Most users find this a non-issue in practice, but it is something to know going
in.

### Durability

This is where the ultralight trade-off lives. The 0.3mm wall is meaningfully thinner than the standard (heavier) TOAKS versions. <cite index="21-15,21-16">With light-gauge titanium pots, they will at some point get bent out of shape — and for pots with a lid, it can be frustrating to repeatedly bend the pot back into shape to get the lid to fit right, only to have it get bent out of shape again.</cite> That said, <cite index="27-15">the pot was tough enough to support a 220 lb adult standing on its end without collapsing it</cite> — which suggests the axial compression strength is fine; it's lateral crushing from pack pressure that's the real risk. Store it inside your pack, not in a side pocket getting squeezed by trekking poles.

On the corrosion side, <cite index="24-15,24-16">one user put their pot atop thousands of campfires and rested it above many alcohol stoves — it ended up coated with creosote but remained faithful.</cite> Titanium rusting is simply not a concern here.

### Packability and Nesting

This is where the 550's design genuinely shines. <cite index="1-1,1-2">Even at 550ml, it's still big enough to contain an entire solo cook kit — a 4 oz fuel canister, a BRS-3000T mini stove, a folding titanium spoon, and a lighter</cite> all nest inside it. <cite index="10-6">A useful pro tip: place a small towel in the bottom of the pot to eliminate fuel canister rattle while hiking and prevent a rust ring from forming inside the pot.</cite>

### Volume Markings

<cite index="11-11">
  The engraved ounce and milliliter markings let you measure precisely and
  conserve water.
</cite>
These markings are stamped into both the inside and outside of the pot, and
they're readable enough in decent light — a practical touch that earns its
weight on trips where water is rationed.

## Pros & Cons

**Pros**

- Extremely light: 60g pot-only, 72g with lid — hard to beat at this price
- Pure titanium means no rust, no coating to flake, no metallic taste
- Doubles convincingly as a mug or bowl
- <cite index="1-9">
    Large enough to nest a 4 oz fuel canister and the rest of a solo cook kit
  </cite>
- Engraved volume markings in both oz and ml
- Lockable lid handle is a genuinely useful detail
- Compatible with every stove type (canister, alcohol, Esbit, wood)

**Cons**

- Uninsulated bare-titanium handles get hot fast — no grip texture, no padding
- Lid fits loosely by design; some users find this frustrating
- 0.3mm walls dent and deform under lateral pack pressure
- <cite index="20-5">Handles are stout but just a bit short</cite> — grabbing
  with cold or gloved hands takes care
- Not a cooking pot in any meaningful sense — it's a boil-water vessel
- No insulation means heat retention after cooking is poor; a cozy helps

## Who Should Buy This

This pot is built for the solo gram-counter who runs a simple boil-water system: canister stove or Esbit, freeze-dried meals, instant coffee. <cite index="16-6">At 2.32 cups, it's just big enough to boil water for a freeze-dried meal and small enough to be used as a cup.</cite> If that describes your camp kitchen — and it describes the kitchen of a lot of thru-hikers and weekend UL backpackers — there's very little reason to carry anything heavier. If you're cooking for two, or you actually want to simmer something, look at the TOAKS 750ml or 900ml instead.

## Verdict

The TOAKS POT-550-L has been a benchmark in the ultralight community for good reason: it does one thing extraordinarily well. At 72g all-in, the price-to-weight ratio is difficult to argue with, and the design decisions — lockable lid, nesting geometry, engraved volume marks — show more thoughtfulness than the stripped-down aesthetic suggests. The loose lid and uninsulated handles are real trade-offs, not deal-breakers. For a solo hiker who boils water and gets out of camp fast, this pot earns a **9/10**.

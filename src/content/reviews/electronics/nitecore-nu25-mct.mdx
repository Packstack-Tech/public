## Overview

The Nitecore NU25 MCT is the third generation of what has become one of the most trusted ultralight headlamps in the thru-hiking community. <cite index="11-21,11-22">The Trek calls the NU25 the all-around best headlamp for thru-hikers, and this third-gen MCT UL builds on that reputation with three color temperatures and a battery Nitecore claims lasts 25 percent longer on its 6-lumen ultralow setting.</cite> The headline upgrade this time around is the MCT (Multiple Color Temperature) system, which lets you switch between warm, neutral, and cool white light — a genuinely useful feature, not just a spec sheet novelty. <cite index="10-23">The standard NU25 MCT weighs 1.76 ounces (50 grams), while the UL version shaves that to 1.66 ounces (47 grams) by swapping the solid headband for a cord strap.</cite> This review covers the standard MCT with the solid headband.

## Performance

### Beam & Color Temperature

The MCT system is the biggest design shift from prior NU25 generations. <cite index="14-4,14-5,14-6,14-7,14-8">The original NU25 had two LEDs — one for throw, one for flood — both with the same color temperature. The MCT drops the separate spot/flood option entirely; both LEDs are always active together.</cite> <cite index="14-9,14-10,14-11,14-12">The combined beam is a hybrid of flood and throw, but leans a bit more toward throw. The two TIR optics let you choose between cool, neutral, or warm white.</cite> That's a real trade-off worth knowing: if you relied on the previous gen's wide flood mode for campsite tasks or close-up reading, the MCT's beam character is noticeably more directional.

On the color temperature side, <cite index="13-22">the 3000K warm light excels in fog, rain, and snow by reducing glare and backscatter; 4500K neutral white is a versatile, natural light for most conditions; and 6500K cool white delivers bright, crisp illumination that works well for extended night hiking.</cite> In practice, the warm setting earns its keep on rainy shoulder-season nights — fewer halos, better contrast on wet trail surfaces. The 6500K is noticeably punchier and the one you'll want when you're pushing miles after dark. <cite index="7-1">The beam covers a 100° wide illumination angle with a peak of 400 lumens and a maximum throw of 132 meters.</cite>

One honest caveat: <cite index="4-31,4-32">the NU25 MCT does not offer a high-CRI option — the LEDs measure at low CRI across all three color temps.</cite> For camp chores or map reading, that's fine. For anything requiring accurate color rendering — identifying plants, assessing a wound — you'll notice the difference compared to a high-CRI option.

### Battery & Charging

<cite index="7-11">
  The NU25 MCT is powered by a built-in 700 mAh lithium-ion battery with a
  maximum runtime of 45 hours, and charges fully in 1 hour and 15 minutes via
  the built-in USB-C port.
</cite>
<cite index="14-26,14-27">
  That 700 mAh capacity isn't large, which is why sustained high-output runtime
  is relatively short.
</cite>
The 45-hour figure is real but lives at the 6-lumen ultralow setting — fine for
stumbling to the privy at 2 a.m., not useful for hiking technical terrain. At
high (400 lm), you're looking at roughly 2.5 hours. Plan accordingly on
multi-day trips without power access.

<cite index="3-21,3-22,3-23">
  The NU25 has a four-LED battery meter on the top of the unit — a quick press
  of the power button when the light is off shows your remaining charge.
  Charging is via USB-C, and all four indicator LEDs illuminate when fully
  charged.
</cite>
The non-replaceable battery is the one long-trip vulnerability: if it dies in
the field, that's that. Carry a small power bank and top up every night or two
on extended trips.

### Fit, Comfort & Build

<cite index="12-23,12-24,12-25">
  The torch body is hard polycarbonate housing a four-LED array: twin spot/flood
  MCT LEDs and two red-light LEDs. Despite its size, it's a robust bit of kit
  with an IP66 rating — completely dustproof and rainproof.
</cite>
<cite index="8-18,8-19">
  In one test, the headlamp was taken down to 22°F and woke up covered in
  condensation — and worked perfectly through it.
</cite>

<cite index="7-6">
  The NU25 MCT features five-step tilt adjustment with the chosen position
  staying put even when your head is moving.
</cite>
<cite index="10-27">
  The main light body can also be removed from the bracket and used as a
  handheld flashlight
</cite>
— handy for finding gear at the bottom of a pack or reading in the tent.
<cite index="11-17">
  The headlamp also ships with a diffusing stuff sack that doubles as a lantern
  when the headlamp is turned on and stuffed inside.
</cite>
That's an unexpectedly practical bonus for tent living.

### Controls & UI

<cite index="16-14">
  Two buttons manage the whole system: four levels of primary white light, three
  special modes (SOS, Beacon, Slow Red Flashing), and two levels of auxiliary
  red light.
</cite>
<cite index="10-9">
  The two buttons are noticeably different in size, which makes it easier to
  identify which you're pressing without looking.
</cite>
That's a real improvement over earlier generations. Still,
<cite index="12-7">
  mastering the full array of functions takes some practice
</cite>
, and first-time users should read the manual before heading into the
backcountry.
<cite index="12-8">The buttons can also be fiddly with gloves</cite> — something
to consider if you're doing a lot of cold-weather hiking.

<cite index="16-17">
  To prevent accidental activation, the NU25 MCT has an electronic lockout mode.
</cite>
<cite index="10-18,10-19">
  Long-pressing both buttons simultaneously locks the light, and the beam
  flashes three times to confirm lockout; pressing any single button during
  lockout produces one flash as a reminder.
</cite>

## Pros & Cons

**Pros**

- At 50g with the standard headband, it competes with almost nothing else at this feature level
- Three color temperatures are genuinely useful, not gimmicky — warm light in rain and fog is a legitimate performance difference
- <cite index="4-20">Charges from empty in 1 hour and 15 minutes</cite> —
  fastest charge in class for a light this size
- IP66 keeps it running through rain, dust, and condensation
- <cite index="11-17">
    Included diffusing stuff sack doubles as a tent lantern
  </cite>
- Two distinctly sized buttons improve heads-on usability over earlier NU25 generations
- Detachable light body adds flexibility as a handheld torch
- Strong value at ~$37

**Cons**

- <cite index="14-13,14-14">
    No high-CRI option — all color temps deliver low CRI
  </cite>
- 700 mAh battery limits high-output runtime to ~2.5 hours; requires disciplined power management on longer trips
- The separate spot/flood beam option from previous generations is gone — the MCT beam leans throwy
- <cite index="12-8">Button controls become awkward with gloves</cite>
- Non-replaceable internal battery is a field failure point with no workaround
- <cite index="3-30,3-31,3-32">
    The USB-C port rubber cover can be difficult to reseat fully once opened
  </cite>
  — a minor but recurring annoyance
- UI complexity requires a manual read before your first night out

## Who Should Buy This

<cite index="13-10,13-11">
  The NU25 MCT is built for hikers and runners who value simplicity, comfort,
  and reliability without unnecessary weight — a lamp that practically
  disappears on your head until you need it.
</cite>
It's the obvious choice for thru-hikers and ultralight backpackers who want a
sub-2 oz rechargeable with more beam flexibility than a basic single-temperature
light. The warm-white mode makes it a better all-conditions tool than most
competitors at this weight.
<cite index="13-23">
  It also makes an excellent backup headlamp on longer expeditions due to its
  low weight.
</cite>
If you regularly need high output for extended technical night hiking (3+
hours), you'll want to pair it with a power bank or look at a heavier lamp with
a bigger cell.

## Verdict

<cite index="6-4">
  The NU25 MCT is the lightest and longest-lasting generation in the NU25 lineup
</cite>
, and after three well-regarded generations, that's saying something. The MCT
color temperature system is the most meaningful spec upgrade in this version —
the 3000K warm mode in rain and the 6500K punch for night hiking aren't
marketing fluff. The trade-offs are real (low CRI, short high-output runtime, no
field-swappable battery) but they're the same trade-offs you accept with any
sub-50g light. At around $37, the price-to-performance ratio is hard to argue
with. **Rating: 8.5/10.**
